A Year in Review – Most Common Sources of Compliance Issues

by Luke Atkinson

Share:
Facebook/LinkedInX

Jump to

Members of the Institute of Public Accountants (IPA) are required to hold themselves accountable to the high ethical and professional standards that the public expects of them. Yet this is not always the case, and when members do fail to meet these expectations, a compliance matter is spawned.

From January to December of 2025, IPA received 65 complaints alleging breaches of relevant ethical and professional standards by IPA members, ranging from complaints lodged from the public to regulator decisions and IPA internal referrals. Across these complaints, patterns begin to emerge about what often triggers the lodging of a complaint, and from these, important lessons in care and diligence can be derived.

Common Cause 1: Failure to Meet Professional Standards

Easily the most common source for compliance issues with practicing accountants are failures to meet professional standards.

All IPA members are required by the Constitution to hold themselves and their professional conduct to the standards outlined by statutory regulators, such as the Accounting Professional & Ethical Standards Board (APESB), and the IPA’s own internal expectations. These standards can seem like red tape from time to time, but it is important to recognise why they are there: when it comes to financial and accounting services, the public needs to be assured that they are in safe hands. Because when they are not, disaster can strike.

It is critical that IPA members stay up to date with all professional standards expected of them, not just for their sake as respected professionals, but for the wellbeing of the entire community.

Common Cause 2: Failure to complete the Professional Practice Program

As an issue the IPA compliance and assurance standards team are all too familiar with, the second most common source of compliance related issues comes from a failure to complete the IPA’s Professional Practice Program (PPP) and/or the Quality Review Program (QRP).

When IPA members are issued a Professional Practice Certificate (PPC), IPA trusts that members will fulfil their professional and ethical obligations by undertaking PPP and QRP within the expected timeframes. For the vast majority of PPC holders, this is the case; yet there a few who do not. Members who fail to comply with these obligations risk having the PCCs revoked – after all, to participate in public practice is a privilege, not a right – or, in severe cases, can have their IPA memberships forfeited.

When IPA members become PPC holders, their greater professional standing comes with greater professional responsibilities. Staying on top of these responsibilities as soon as they arise is the easiest way to avoid that dreaded call from the compliance team.

Common Cause 3: Poor Communication

Another common source for compliance issues is poor communication.

Thankfully, this is a very easy issue to fix. Staying up to date on correspondence, while it may be difficult, especially for smaller practices, is as simple as returning a call, even if there is nothing of importance to be said just yet. Maintaining an open line of communication will ease any worries in a client’s mind and prevent any unnecessary complaints being raised with IPA.

Common Cause 4: Failure to Provide Services

IPA members in public practices are required under the standards of APES 305 to document and communicate to their clients the terms of their professional engagement. Familiarising oneself with these terms is the simplest way to avoid any undue confusion that might arise when a client believes designated services are not being provided.

Takeaways

While these are just a few of many sources, IPA’s 2025 complaints highlight a clear message: professionalism, communication, and meeting core obligations matter. Staying informed, responsive, and diligent protects clients, the community, and the reputation of every practitioner.
